Activity 1: Hybrid Biogas-Wind Energy System

Rural Health Clinic Power Solution

‚ö†Ô∏è Problem Statement

Rural health clinics in developing regions face critical challenges with unreliable electricity supply, affecting medical equipment operation, vaccine storage, and emergency lighting. Traditional grid connections are often unavailable or unreliable in remote agricultural areas, while single renewable energy sources suffer from intermittency issues. A reliable, continuous power solution is essential for 24/7 healthcare operations.

üìã Overview

This hybrid renewable energy system combines biogas generation from agricultural waste with wind energy to create a reliable, sustainable power solution for rural health clinics. 1. System Overview

This hybrid renewable energy system integrates biogas generation from organic waste with wind turbine technology to provide continuous, reliable electrical power for rural health clinics. The system addresses the critical challenge of energy intermittency by combining the consistent baseload power from biogas with the variable output from wind energy, ensuring 24/7 availability of electricity for medical equipment, lighting, refrigeration, and other essential healthcare operations. The design incorporates battery storage and sophisticated power conditioning equipment to maintain stable power supply even during periods of low wind or biogas production.

4. Energy Conversion Process

Source of Energy

The system harnesses energy from two renewable sources: biogas produced through anaerobic digestion of agricultural and organic waste, and kinetic energy from wind.

Conversion Process

In the biogas pathway, organic matter undergoes bacterial decomposition in an oxygen-free environment at 40-42¬∞C, producing biogas containing approximately 60% methane. This biogas fuels a combined heat and power (CHP) engine generating 40-50 kW of electricity and 69-86 kW of thermal energy. Simultaneously, wind turbines capture kinetic energy from moving air masses, converting it to mechanical rotation and then to electrical energy through AC generators. A sophisticated hybrid controller integrates both sources, managing load distribution and storing excess energy in a 48V battery bank.

Output/Utilization

The system produces over 327 kWh daily for medical equipment, lighting, and refrigeration, while thermal energy provides space heating and hot water.

Real-world Application

This configuration is particularly valuable for rural health clinics as it ensures continuous power availability‚Äîbiogas provides reliable baseload power while wind energy supplements during favorable conditions, addressing the critical need for 24/7 healthcare operations in off-grid areas with abundant agricultural waste.

Activity 2: Tidal Power Plant

Coastal Village Energy Solution

‚ö†Ô∏è Problem Statement

Coastal villages often lack reliable electricity infrastructure while facing challenges from climate change and rising sea levels. Traditional power generation requires fuel transport to remote locations, increasing costs and environmental impact. These communities need sustainable, predictable energy solutions that can operate in marine environments.

üìã Overview

A tidal power plant harnesses the predictable rise and fall of ocean tides to generate clean electricity for coastal communities. Unlike solar and wind, tidal energy is highly predictable, providing reliable baseload power while offering additional benefits like coastal protection and transportation infrastructure.

1. System Overview

This tidal power plant design utilizes a barrage-type system to harness the immense energy stored in ocean tides for electricity generation. The system is specifically designed for coastal villages with substantial tidal ranges (minimum 4-5 meters), converting the predictable rise and fall of ocean water levels into reliable electrical power. Unlike intermittent renewable sources such as wind and solar, tidal energy offers exceptional predictability as tides can be accurately forecast years in advance based on lunar and solar cycles. The barrage structure serves multiple purposes including power generation, coastal protection, and potential aquaculture opportunities, making it an integrated coastal infrastructure solution.

4. Energy Conversion Process

Source of Energy

Tidal range (minimum 4-5m) created by gravitational pull of the moon and sun on Earth's oceans.

Conversion Process

Rising tide impounds water behind the barrage creating a head difference (5.82m rated). Water is released through bulb turbines embedded in the barrage structure, converting hydraulic potential energy to mechanical rotation at 64.3 rpm. The turbine directly drives a 25.4 MW generator producing electricity at 10.2 kV, which is stepped up via transformers for grid transmission.

Output/Utilization

25.4 MW per turbine unit, 550 GWh annual energy for village distribution. The system operates 2 cycles per day corresponding to tidal patterns.

Real-world Application

Predictable and reliable (unlike wind/solar), suitable for coastal villages with large tidal range, provides base-load power. Examples include Sihwa Lake (254 MW) in South Korea demonstrating commercial viability for coastal communities.

Activity 3: Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion (OTEC)

Dual-Purpose Island Energy System

‚ö†Ô∏è Problem Statement

Tropical islands face dual challenges of energy poverty and freshwater scarcity, often relying on expensive imported fossil fuels and energy-intensive desalination. These remote locations typically lack conventional energy resources but have abundant ocean thermal gradients that remain untapped.

üìã Overview

OTEC systems exploit temperature differences between warm surface water and cold deep ocean water to simultaneously generate electricity and produce freshwater. This innovative technology addresses both energy and water security for tropical island communities while providing continuous baseload power independent of weather conditions.

1. System Overview

Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion (OTEC) represents an innovative approach to sustainable energy generation that exploits the temperature difference between warm surface waters and cold deep ocean waters in tropical regions. This system offers a unique dual benefit: continuous base-load electricity generation and concurrent production of desalinated freshwater, addressing two critical resource challenges faced by tropical island communities. Unlike solar and wind systems that are subject to weather variations, OTEC provides highly reliable, continuous power with capacity factors approaching 90%, making it ideal for base-load applications. The system operates on a closed-cycle principle for power generation using ammonia as a working fluid, while simultaneously employing an open-cycle process for freshwater production.

4. Energy Conversion Process

Source of Energy

Ocean thermal gradient - temperature difference (minimum 20¬∞C) between warm surface water (20-25¬∞C) and cold deep ocean water (4-6¬∞C) at approximately 1000 meters depth.

Conversion Process

Warm surface seawater is pumped through an evaporator heat exchanger, transferring thermal energy to liquid ammonia and causing it to vaporize into high-pressure gas. This pressurized ammonia vapor drives a turbine-generator set (100 kW in pilot installations, scalable to megawatt capacities), converting thermal energy to mechanical rotation and then to electricity. After passing through the turbine, the low-pressure ammonia vapor enters a condenser cooled by cold deep ocean water pumped through a massive insulated pipe (10m diameter for 100 MW plants), where it condenses back to liquid form, completing the closed loop. Simultaneously, an open-cycle desalination process flash-evaporates warm seawater in a low-pressure chamber, producing steam that is condensed using cold deep water to yield freshwater.

Output/Utilization

Dual output - electricity generation (100 kW pilot scale, scalable to MW) and desalinated freshwater (58.874 t/day), thermal efficiency 2-25%.

Real-world Application

Ideal for tropical islands with deep ocean access, provides continuous base-load power (high capacity factor ~90%), addresses water scarcity alongside energy needs. Suitable for Hawaii, Philippines, Indonesia, and Pacific islands. Examples include Okinawa pilot plant and Hawaii's 105 kW facility demonstrating technical and commercial feasibility.
